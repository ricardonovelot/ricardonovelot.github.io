June: a photo journal you scroll like a feed

A photo journal for iPhone that takes the scroll mechanics of social apps and points them at your own life. Designed and built solo in Swift and UIKit.

Role
Design and engineering, solo
Platform
iOS, Swift and UIKit
Timeline
2025 to present
Status
TestFlight, App Store release in progress

The idea

Social apps spent a decade making exploration feel effortless. The problem wasn't how they worked, it was what they asked us to consume. June uses those same tools to help you rediscover your own life.

Designing it and building it

I designed June and then built all of it in Swift and UIKit: the feed that assembles itself out of the photo library, the music layer, and the backend that keeps it fluid on seamless features.

Nobody wants to face a blank page

Journaling apps open on an empty screen and a blinking cursor, June never starts from nothing. It opens on your life already arranged: today, yesterday, the trip you took last month, grouped without you asking for it. Writing is one tap away from a photo you already care about, and the prompt is waiting for you: What is making you feel grateful?.

Memory has a soundtrack

Four seconds of the right song and you are back: the car, that summer, who you were sitting next to. A photo takes longer to do that, and sometimes it never gets there at all.

So a collection in June can carry a song. The easy build is to look up what was popular that year and hand you the hits. I tried it. It felt like someone else's memory. What brings a year back is the song you wore out, not the song that charted.

So June reaches for what you actually played, and it lets you set the exact second the song starts, because the bars before the chorus are not the chorus.

Speed is part of the feeling

Most apps put too much friction between you and the thing you meant to do. June doesn't. It acts the moment you touch it, answers with a haptic, and keeps undo within reach. Clearing out an afternoon of bad photos becomes one flowing pass instead of forty taps confirming what you already decided.

A confirmation dialog protects the app from you. An undo button protects you, and costs you nothing on the days you were right the first time.

That principle runs through everything. The thumbnail strip you scrub with your thumb, where the photo under your finger grows so you never lose your place. Pinch one photo to reframe it and a pill offers to do the same to the rest of the collection, because if the framing bothered you once it will bother you again. Tap a heart and it fills instantly, then quietly writes itself into your Apple Photos library in the background, since that bookkeeping is the app's problem and not yours. Even starting a journal entry is a gesture: pull the card sideways and a "+" appears under your thumb, so you never go hunting for a button to write about the day.

Where it is now

June is on TestFlight, with the App Store release in progress. The demo video shows it running.
