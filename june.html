June: a photo journal you scroll like a feed

A photo journal for iPhone that takes the scroll mechanics of social apps and points them at your own life. Designed and built solo in Swift and UIKit.

Role
Design and engineering, solo
Platform
iOS, Swift and UIKit
Timeline
2025 to present
Status
TestFlight, App Store release in progress

The idea

Social apps spent a decade making exploration feel effortless. The problem wasn't how they worked, it was what they asked us to consume. June uses those same tools to help you rediscover your own life instead.

Watch the demo video to see it move.

Designing it and building it

I designed June and then built all of it in Swift and UIKit: the feed that assembles itself out of the photo library, the music layer, and the backend that keeps it fluid on seamless features.

Doing both jobs changed how I designed. When an interaction turned out to be expensive to render, I didn't hand the problem to anyone, I went and fixed what was underneath it.

Nobody wants to face a blank page

Journaling apps open on an empty screen and a blinking cursor, June never starts from nothing. It opens on your life already arranged: today, yesterday, the trip you took last month, grouped without you asking for it. Writing is one tap away from a photo you already care about, and the prompt is waiting for you: What is making you feel grateful? Reflection starts from something instead of from a cursor.

Trust the tap

Most apps stop you to ask if you are sure. June doesn't. It acts the moment you touch it, answers with a haptic, and keeps every action within reach. Clearing out a bad afternoon of photos becomes one flowing pass instead of forty dialogs asking you to confirm the obvious.

An undo button protects you, and costs you nothing on the days you were right the first time. That principle runs through everything, down to the thumbnail strip you scrub with your thumb, where the photo under your finger grows so you never lose your place.

Memory has a soundtrack

A song can put you back in a room faster than a photograph can. So a collection in June can carry one, and a scrubber sets the exact second it starts, because the bars before the chorus are not the chorus. If Shazam hears a song while you are out somewhere, it stays pinned to that day. A whole month can play back as a full screen collage with the music you were listening to at the time.

Speed is part of the feeling

A spinner is fatal in an app like this. Hesitate at the wrong moment and the spell breaks: you stop remembering and start waiting. So June has one rule I refused to bend, which is that you never see a black screen.

Meeting that rule was an engineering problem, not a visual one. Photos come off the disk first, so there is always something to draw. The app learns which way you tend to move through the feed and warms those photos before you arrive. Video playback stays inside the budget of decoders iOS actually gives an app, so the tenth clip opens as fast as the first. None of that appears in a screenshot. You only feel it as the absence of waiting.

Where it is now

June is on TestFlight, with the App Store release in progress. The demo video shows it running.
