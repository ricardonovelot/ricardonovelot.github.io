June: a photo journal you scroll like a feed

A photo journal for iPhone that takes the scroll mechanics of social apps and points them at your own life. Designed and built solo in Swift and UIKit.

Role
Design and engineering, solo
Platform
iOS, Swift and UIKit
Timeline
2025 to present
Status
TestFlight, App Store release in progress

The idea

Social apps spent a decade perfecting the mechanics of scrolling, and the mechanics are genuinely good. The content is what wears you down. June takes those effortless gestures and points them at your own life: your photos, your trips, your notes, instead of a feed of strangers.

Watch the demo video to see it move.

One person, the whole product

I designed June and then wrote all of it in Swift and UIKit. That covers the feed that assembles itself out of the photo library, grouping days and detecting trips. It covers a full Apple Music layer. And it covers the caching and prefetching that keep everything smooth on libraries with tens of thousands of photos. One person made every call here, from the shape of a gesture to the disk format of a thumbnail, and the case for working this way is coherence: the interaction design and the engineering were never allowed to disagree.

Interactions that trust the tap

Most apps interrupt you to confirm. June commits instantly, with a haptic, and makes reversal cheap. Deleting is the clearest example: instead of a confirmation dialog, deletions queue with one tap and undo with one tap. The same rule runs through the app. Toggles respond the moment you touch them and finish their work in the background.

Under each photo carousel sits a thumbnail strip for scrubbing, and the centered thumbnail blooms wider so your position stays legible under your thumb. Pinch switches a photo between fill and fit, with an “apply to all” pill when one answer should win everywhere.

Feelings tint the journal

Journal entries are cards. An entry can carry a feeling picked from a wheel, 16 of them plus neutral, and the feeling tints the card. Neutral entries stay colorless, so color always means something you chose. Writing prompts rotate between a feeling based question like “What is making you feel grateful?”, your own prompts, and affirmations.

Music that stays with the memory

Collections can carry Apple Music songs, and an inline scrubber sets the exact second a song starts, because the right entrance matters. When Shazam recognizes a song, it stays pinned to that collection under “In this collection”. Months can play back as full screen collages with their music.

Engineering as design material

I treat performance as part of the design. June has a hard rule that a black screen is never acceptable. The image pipeline is disk first with an HEIC cache, so there is always something to draw. Prefetching adapts to behavior: a predictor learns your navigation patterns and warms the paths you tend to take next. Video decoding runs on a budget, because iOS hands you a finite pool of hardware decoders and an app that ignores the limit eventually pays for it.

None of this shows up in a screenshot, but it's why the feed never shows a spinner or a black frame.

The small decisions

  • A day cuts at 5 AM instead of midnight, so a late night stays with its evening.
  • Trips are detected automatically and get one carousel per day.
  • The interface is dark only.

Where it is now

June is on TestFlight and the App Store release is in progress. The demo video shows it running.
