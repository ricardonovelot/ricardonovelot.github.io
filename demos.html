Demos: design validation on real devices

An internal iOS app I designed and built in Swift so the design team at Whirlpool can test high fidelity concepts on real devices before they reach the official apps.

Role
Designer and solo developer
Platform
iOS, Swift
Timeline
2025 to present
Users
The Whirlpool UX design team

Context

I am a designer on the UX team at Whirlpool. A design can look resolved in a mockup and still feel different the moment it runs on a real device: the timing, the navigation, how a theme reads on an actual screen. Demos exists to close that gap before anything reaches Whirlpool's official apps.

What it is

Demos replicates actual product experiences at high fidelity so we could put concepts on real devices early. That meant building more than screens. It has its own design system architecture and a custom navigation implementation, because a simulation is only convincing if the connective tissue behaves like the real thing. It switches between the brands in Whirlpool's portfolio and their themes, and it can run multiple variants of a design side by side for comparison. Builds go to the team through TestFlight.

How the team used it

When the team couldn't agree on a direction, we load the options into Demos and compare them on a real phone instead of arguing over screenshots. When a pattern has to hold up across every brand we own, we flip through the themes and watch for the one where it falls apart.

The point is what people are reacting to. A rough prototype gets you opinions about the prototype: the placeholder text, the animation that isn't there yet. Demos is a real app, so the reaction you get is the reaction the customer will have. Everyone installs it once from TestFlight and gets each new build as I push it.

What building it taught the design side

Building the design system as code was the biggest lesson. A component library in a design tool tolerates ambiguity. Code doesn't, so every state and naming decision had to actually be made, and that discipline followed me back into regular design work. The tool's job is to answer design questions, not to become a second product to maintain.

Demos moves the moment you discover a design is wrong to before anything gets built, instead of after it reaches customers.
