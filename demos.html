Demos: design validation on real devices

An internal iOS app I designed and built in Swift so the design team at Whirlpool can test high fidelity concepts on real devices before they reach the official apps.

Role
Designer and solo developer
Platform
iOS, Swift
Timeline

Users
The Whirlpool UX design team

Context

I am a designer on the UX team at Whirlpool. A design can look resolved in a mockup and still feel different the moment it runs on a real device: the timing, the navigation, how a theme reads on an actual screen. Demos exists to close that gap before anything reaches Whirlpool's official apps.

What it is

Demos replicates actual product experiences at high fidelity so the team can put concepts on real devices early. That meant building more than screens. It has its own design system architecture and a custom navigation implementation, because a simulation is only convincing if the connective tissue behaves like the real thing. It switches between the brands in Whirlpool's portfolio and their themes, and it can run multiple variants of a design side by side for comparison. Builds go to the team through TestFlight.

How the team uses it

When a direction is contested, we load the options as variants and compare them on device. Patterns that need to hold across the brand portfolio get flipped through each theme so we can watch where they break. Because Demos behaves like the shipping product, a reaction to it is a reaction to something close to what customers would eventually get, not to a rough stand in. TestFlight keeps distribution simple: install once, get updates as I push them.

What building it taught the design side

I sit on the team Demos serves, so I hit every rough edge in my own work before anyone had to report it.

Building the design system as code was the biggest lesson. A component library in a design tool tolerates ambiguity. Code doesn't, so every state and naming decision had to actually be made, and that discipline followed me back into regular design work. The tool's job is to answer design questions, not to become a second product to maintain.
